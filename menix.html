Understanding Cyberbullying & Online Harassment

Learn how to recognize, prevent, and respond to digital abuse in our connected world.

Learn More

What is Cyberbullying?

Cyberbullying is the use of digital communication tools to deliberately and repeatedly harass, threaten, or humiliate someone. Unlike traditional bullying, cyberbullying can happen 24/7, reach a victim anywhere, and be perpetrated anonymously, making it particularly damaging.

Common Forms of Cyberbullying

  • Harassment: Sending offensive, rude, and insulting messages
  • Denigration: Spreading rumors or posting false information
  • Impersonation: Pretending to be someone else to damage their reputation
  • Outing: Sharing someone's private information without consent
  • Exclusion: Intentionally leaving someone out of online groups or activities
  • Cyberstalking: Repeated, intense harassment that includes threats

The Impact of Cyberbullying

Cyberbullying can have severe consequences on victims' mental health, academic performance, and overall well-being. The constant nature of digital harassment means victims often feel they have no escape, leading to feelings of powerlessness and isolation.

Psychological Effects

Victims of cyberbullying often experience increased rates of:

  • Anxiety and depression
  • Low self-esteem
  • Social isolation
  • Sleep disturbances
  • Academic difficulties
  • In extreme cases, suicidal thoughts

Societal Impact

Beyond individual victims, cyberbullying contributes to a toxic online environment that affects everyone. It can:

  • Reduce participation in online spaces, particularly among vulnerable groups
  • Create environments where harassment becomes normalized
  • Undermine digital platforms as spaces for healthy discourse
  • Place additional burdens on schools, workplaces, and law enforcement

Quick Facts

  • About 37% of young people between 12 and 17 have been bullied online
  • Girls are more likely than boys to be victims of cyberbullying
  • 60% of teens have witnessed online bullying
  • Only 1 in 10 teen victims will inform a parent or trusted adult
  • Victims of cyberbullying are 2x more likely to attempt suicide

Prevention Tips

  • Think before you post online
  • Never share passwords with friends
  • Adjust privacy settings on social media
  • Save evidence of cyberbullying
  • Talk to a trusted adult if you're being harassed
  • Be an upstander, not a bystander

Get Help Now

If you or someone you know is being cyberbullied:

  • Crisis Text Line: Text HOME to 741741
  • National Suicide Prevention Lifeline: 1-800-273-8255
  • StopBullying.gov: Report cyberbullying
